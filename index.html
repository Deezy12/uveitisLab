ARVO 2026 · Poster #0637-512

Longitudinal Analysis of Ellipsoid Zone Loss in Stargardt Disease

How do ellipsoid zone (EZ) loss, DDAF, and TDAF areas progress over time in genetically confirmed Stargardt disease, and how does progression vary by genotype severity?
Central finding: EZ loss, DDAF, and TDAF areas all progressed significantly across G1–G3, with rates increasing monotonically with genotype severity. EZ loss showed the most consistent and earliest signal — G3 progressed approximately 3.5× faster than G1 — and baseline √EZ loss independently predicted future TDAF enlargement.

Impact of Pharmacologic Dilation on Volumetric Anterior Segment Optical Coherence Tomography Quantification of Anterior Chamber Cell Density in Uveitis Patients

What is the effect of pharmacologic dilation on clinical and volumetric AS-OCT quantification of anterior chamber inflammation in uveitis?
Central finding: Pharmacologic dilation does not significantly alter anterior chamber cell density or clinical SUN grade. However, dilation-induced pigment dispersion can affect AS-OCT hyperreflective particle quantification, particularly in eyes with greater pigment release.

Methods
1
Study DesignProspective case series at a single uveitis referral center (Cole Eye Institute, Cleveland Clinic).

Discussion
🔬
Pigment Dispersion
Pharmacologic dilation can liberate iris pigment into the anterior chamber. AS-OCT threshold-based detection cannot distinguish pigment granules from inflammatory cells, producing spurious density elevations — particularly in eyes with dense brown irides or posterior synechiae.
💧
Aqueous Volume Dilution
Dilation transiently increases AC fluid volume. The same number of particles distributed across a larger AS-OCT scan volume registers as lower apparent density, likely explaining the density decreases observed in most eyes without significant pigment release.
⚖️
Clinical Grade Stability
SUN grading was unchanged in 95% of eyes post-dilation, confirming that slit-lamp clinical assessment is robust to pharmacologic dilation. The two modalities capture overlapping but mechanistically distinct aspects of anterior chamber inflammation.
📊
Implications for Monitoring
Pre-dilation AS-OCT imaging should be prioritized for serial monitoring — especially in patients with brown irides, a history of pigment dispersion, or elevated baseline pigment grade — to avoid overestimating inflammatory activity.
Limitations
  • AS-OCT quantification cannot distinguish inflammatory hyperreflective particles from pigment-derived particles — a fundamental limitation of all threshold-based detection algorithms.
  • Dilation may transiently increase AC fluid volume, diluting apparent particle density in the fixed AS-OCT scan volume; this confounds pre-to-post comparisons in both directions.
  • Single-center prospective case series; findings may not generalize to different dilation agents, imaging devices, or patient populations with varying disease activity.
  • Subgroup analyses of outlier eyes with high pigment release were limited by small sample size within this group (n = 9 with large pigment change ≥1.0 grade).

GLP-1 Receptor Agonist Use and Risk of NAION in Type 2 Diabetes

What is the association between GLP-1RA use and incident non-arteritic anterior ischemic optic neuropathy compared to alternative antidiabetic medications among patients with type 2 diabetes?
Central finding: New GLP-1RA users showed moderately greater NAION incidence compared to new SGLT2i users (HR 1.41); comparisons with DPP-4i and sulfonylureas were directionally consistent but statistically non-significant.

Background
  • Non-arteritic anterior ischemic optic neuropathy (NAION) is an important cause of acute vision loss among older adults, and GLP-1 receptor agonists are increasingly prescribed for type 2 diabetes, obesity, and other indications.
  • An earlier neuro-ophthalmology cohort reported a markedly higher NAION rate among semaglutide users (HR 4.28, 95% CI 1.62–11.29), with subsequent national registry data suggesting elevated risk at more modest effect sizes.
  • A recent multi-center analysis with pairwise drug comparisons found significant effects only between semaglutide and empagliflozin — motivating an active-comparator design at scale.
Methods
1
Data SourceEpic Cosmos — federated, de-identified electronic health records database with over 300 million patients across the United States
2
DesignRetrospective, active-comparator, new-user cohort study with first record of GLP-1RA, SGLT2i, DPP-4i, or sulfonylurea between January 2021 and December 2024 in patients with prior T2D
3
GLP-1RAs includedSemaglutide, liraglutide, dulaglutide, lixisenatide, exenatide, albiglutide. Patients with prior NAION or other non-insulin second-line antidiabetic agents were excluded
4
Primary OutcomeIncident NAION (ICD-10-CM H47.01) analyzed as time-to-event — censoring at death, giant cell arteritis, last encounter date, or 5 years
5
Cohort BalancingThree pairwise drug class comparisons; missing continuous variables multiply imputed (m=20) via MICE. Propensity scores estimated using ridge regression with overlap weighting on demographics, comorbidities, medications, and healthcare utilization
6
Statistical AnalysisPooled hazard ratios calculated from Cox proportional hazards models using Rubin's rules. After weighting, all pairwise standardized mean differences were < 0.10

Notable Baseline Imbalances (Pre-Weighting)

GLP-1RA initiators were younger (mean 56.2 vs. 64.6 years for SGLT2i), more frequently female (62.7% vs. 42.8%), had higher BMI (38.7 vs. 33.0), and lower HbA1c (7.27% vs. 7.70%). They also had lower rates of cardiovascular disease, heart failure, and chronic kidney disease — reflecting distinct prescribing patterns. After overlap weighting, all SMDs were < 0.10.

Discussion
📊
Consistent Pattern
This novel use of Epic Cosmos comparing drug classes shows a similar pattern to previously reported pairwise comparisons of individual medications, supporting the SGLT2i comparison as the most informative signal.
⚖️
Modest Absolute Risk
While findings contribute to a growing body of literature on ophthalmic effects of GLP-1RAs, absolute NAION risks remain low and the hazard ratios reported here are more moderate than often previously described.
🔬
Active-Comparator Design
Using new-user cohorts with active comparators reduces confounding by indication versus non-user designs. Overlap weighting targets patients similarly likely to receive either treatment based on measured covariates.
💧
Class-Level Signal
Effects across DPP-4i and sulfonylurea comparators were directionally consistent but non-significant, suggesting any elevated risk may be specific to comparison with SGLT2i — itself a class with potential ocular protective effects.
Limitations
  • Inadequate capture of NAION from ICD-10 diagnosis codes alone may introduce outcome misclassification.
  • Intention-to-treat analysis based on initial prescription dilutes true on-treatment effects through medication discontinuation and switching.
  • Residual confounding remains plausible despite overlap weighting — particularly for unmeasured factors such as disease duration, adherence, and inflammatory biomarkers.
  • Substantial baseline imbalances across cohorts (age, sex, BMI, comorbidity burden) reflect distinct prescribing patterns; weighting balances measured but not unmeasured covariates.
  • Retrospective design precludes causal inference, and absolute event rates may be overestimated due to variable follow-up duration.

Proposed Mechanisms
🔬
Anti-Inflammatory
GLP-1RAs reduce IL-6, IL-1β, and TNF-α — key cytokines implicated in ocular surface inflammation and DED pathogenesis.
💧
Lacrimal Gland
GLP-1 receptors are expressed in lacrimal gland and ocular adnexal tissues. Topical liraglutide improved tear secretion in diabetic murine models; human studies report higher Schirmer values and longer TBUT.
⚖️
Metabolic & Lifestyle
GLP-1RA-associated weight loss and lifestyle modification may reduce DED severity. The stronger signal in non-diabetic patients — where hyperglycemia is absent — supports this as an important driver.
Limitations
  • ICD-10 code-based DED classification may introduce misclassification bias, as dry eye diagnoses are clinically heterogeneous and often assigned by exclusion.
  • Despite propensity matching, BMI and HbA1c remained imbalanced (SMD >0.10) across cohorts, indicating potential residual confounding by metabolic severity.
  • Confounding by indication is plausible — GLP-1RAs, insulin, metformin, and SGLT2i are prescribed to distinct patient profiles with different baseline risk.
  • Unmeasured confounders (disease duration, adherence, inflammatory biomarkers, socioeconomic status, concurrent immunomodulatory therapy) were not captured.
  • No clinical exam metrics were available (TBUT, tear osmolarity, meibography, corneal esthesiometry), limiting clinical granularity.
  • Retrospective design precludes causal inference. An ophthalmology encounter as the index event may introduce immortal time bias.

Background

Fundus autofluorescence (FAF) is widely used to grade retinal disease lesions in clinical care and as a clinical trial endpoint, but current grading suffers from intergrader variability — especially when defining partial signal loss boundaries and consistent intensity thresholds for lesion edges. Existing methods rely on freehand subjective segmentation and lack standardization. To address these limitations, we developed a semiautomated analysis tool that applies reference-based thresholds to standardize lesion quantification and improve objectivity, reproducibility, and annotation efficiency for research and longitudinal monitoring.

Methods
1
Tool DevelopmentPython-based FAF analysis interface with user-guided area calibration, reference-based darkness thresholding, and freehand boundary tools with thresholding restricted to selected areas
2
Reference ThresholdLesion detection uses a reference darkness derived from a user-selected pixel representing the darkest vessel segment over the optic disc; users define minimum lesion intensity as a percentage of this reference
3
Boundary & Editing ToolsFreehand outlining with thresholding restricted to the selected area, plus an eraser tool for removal of vessels and artifacts within the region of interest
4
Validation Cohort20 eyes with Stargardt disease graded by three trained graders using both the semiautomated tool and a manual Microsoft Paint workflow
5
Outcome MeasuresDDAF (definitely decreased autofluorescence), QDAF (questionably decreased autofluorescence), and TDAF (total decreased autofluorescence) areas — segmented with both methods and compared via Dice similarity coefficients
6
Statistical AnalysisIntergrader agreement compared between methods using Dice similarity coefficients; annotation time recorded per eye for each workflow

Discussion
🎯
Reference-Based Thresholding
Anchoring lesion intensity to a per-image reference (darkest disc-vessel pixel) standardizes thresholds across images, scanners, and gain settings — removing a major source of intergrader variability.
📊
Largest Gains for Partial Signal
QDAF showed the most dramatic improvement (Dice 0.44 → 0.80) — exactly where subjective grading struggles most: the gradient zone between normal and definitely decreased autofluorescence.
⚙️
Workflow Efficiency
A 25% reduction in annotation time per eye, with a tighter range (7.2–8.1 vs 8.4–11.9 min), supports scalability for clinical trials and longitudinal monitoring of dystrophic and atrophic retinal diseases.
🔬
Trial & Clinical Implications
More reproducible boundary delineation directly improves the signal-to-noise ratio for FAF-based endpoints — narrowing required sample sizes and enabling more confident detection of treatment effects in retinal disease trials.
Limitations
  • Validation limited to 20 Stargardt eyes; performance in other dystrophic and atrophic retinal diseases (geographic atrophy, retinitis pigmentosa) requires further study.
  • Three-grader cohort; broader multi-center, multi-grader validation would strengthen generalizability of the agreement metrics.
  • Reference pixel selection still introduces a small subjective component; future work could fully automate disc-vessel reference detection.
  • Dice agreement does not capture systematic bias — additional metrics (e.g. Bland-Altman of lesion area, sensitivity to longitudinal change) are needed for trial-endpoint qualification.
  • DDAF improvement did not reach statistical significance (p = 0.13), likely reflecting the smaller, sharply demarcated nature of DDAF lesions for which manual grading already performs reasonably well.
