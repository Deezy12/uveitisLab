Longitudinal Analysis of Ellipsoid Zone Loss in Stargardt Disease

How do ellipsoid zone (EZ) loss, DDAF, and TDAF areas progress over time in genetically confirmed Stargardt disease, and how does progression vary by genotype severity?
Central finding: EZ loss, DDAF, and TDAF areas all progressed significantly across G1–G3, with rates increasing monotonically with genotype severity; EZ loss showed the most consistent and earliest signal, with G3 progressing 3.5× faster than G1.

Discussion
🔬
Genotype Drives Progression
Genotype severity was the strongest and most consistent predictor across all three outcomes after FDR correction — supporting molecular classification as a key prognostic axis.
📊
EZ Loss as Early Marker
Baseline √EZ loss predicted future TDAF progression, suggesting EZ disruption precedes and forecasts subsequent atrophic enlargement on FAF.
⚖️
Square-Root Scaling
Square-root transformation linearizes the radial spread of atrophic lesions and yields stable progression rates across the dynamic range — consistent with prior work in geographic atrophy.
💧
Functional Correlation
Larger baseline lesion areas tracked with lower BCVA, and longitudinal TDAF and EZ loss change correlated with BCVA decline — supporting their use as structural surrogates for visual function.
Limitations
  • Informative baseline censoring excluded 75% of G4 eyes from progression analyses. Remaining G4 follow-up data derive from a single patient whose eyes — by virtue of not being censored — represent the least severely affected within G4, biasing any progression estimate low.
  • Modest cohort size (54 eyes / 27 patients) limits power for subgroup analyses, particularly within smaller genotype strata (G3 and G4).
  • The base_flecks_bin predictor model failed to converge and was excluded from all predictor tables, leaving the role of fundus flecks unresolved.
  • Single-center data may not generalize to broader Stargardt populations; multicenter validation is warranted, especially for genotype severity classification.
  • Analyses are observational; rate estimates may not directly translate to expected on-treatment effects in interventional settings.

GLP-1 Receptor Agonist Use and Risk of NAION in Type 2 Diabetes

What is the association between GLP-1RA use and incident non-arteritic anterior ischemic optic neuropathy compared to alternative antidiabetic medications among patients with type 2 diabetes?
Central finding: New GLP-1RA users showed moderately greater NAION incidence compared to new SGLT2i users (HR 1.41); comparisons with DPP-4i and sulfonylureas were directionally consistent but statistically non-significant.

Background
  • Non-arteritic anterior ischemic optic neuropathy (NAION) is an important cause of acute vision loss among older adults, and GLP-1 receptor agonists are increasingly prescribed for type 2 diabetes, obesity, and other indications.
  • An earlier neuro-ophthalmology cohort reported a markedly higher NAION rate among semaglutide users (HR 4.28, 95% CI 1.62–11.29), with subsequent national registry data suggesting elevated risk at more modest effect sizes.
  • A recent multi-center analysis with pairwise drug comparisons found significant effects only between semaglutide and empagliflozin — motivating an active-comparator design at scale.
Methods
1
Data SourceEpic Cosmos — federated, de-identified electronic health records database with over 300 million patients across the United States
2
DesignRetrospective, active-comparator, new-user cohort study with first record of GLP-1RA, SGLT2i, DPP-4i, or sulfonylurea between January 2021 and December 2024 in patients with prior T2D
3
GLP-1RAs includedSemaglutide, liraglutide, dulaglutide, lixisenatide, exenatide, albiglutide. Patients with prior NAION or other non-insulin second-line antidiabetic agents were excluded
4
Primary OutcomeIncident NAION (ICD-10-CM H47.01) analyzed as time-to-event — censoring at death, giant cell arteritis, last encounter date, or 5 years
5
Cohort BalancingThree pairwise drug class comparisons; missing continuous variables multiply imputed (m=20) via MICE. Propensity scores estimated using ridge regression with overlap weighting on demographics, comorbidities, medications, and healthcare utilization
6
Statistical AnalysisPooled hazard ratios calculated from Cox proportional hazards models using Rubin's rules. After weighting, all pairwise standardized mean differences were < 0.10

Notable Baseline Imbalances (Pre-Weighting)

GLP-1RA initiators were younger (mean 56.2 vs. 64.6 years for SGLT2i), more frequently female (62.7% vs. 42.8%), had higher BMI (38.7 vs. 33.0), and lower HbA1c (7.27% vs. 7.70%). They also had lower rates of cardiovascular disease, heart failure, and chronic kidney disease — reflecting distinct prescribing patterns. After overlap weighting, all SMDs were < 0.10.

Discussion
📊
Consistent Pattern
This novel use of Epic Cosmos comparing drug classes shows a similar pattern to previously reported pairwise comparisons of individual medications, supporting the SGLT2i comparison as the most informative signal.
⚖️
Modest Absolute Risk
While findings contribute to a growing body of literature on ophthalmic effects of GLP-1RAs, absolute NAION risks remain low and the hazard ratios reported here are more moderate than often previously described.
🔬
Active-Comparator Design
Using new-user cohorts with active comparators reduces confounding by indication versus non-user designs. Overlap weighting targets patients similarly likely to receive either treatment based on measured covariates.
💧
Class-Level Signal
Effects across DPP-4i and sulfonylurea comparators were directionally consistent but non-significant, suggesting any elevated risk may be specific to comparison with SGLT2i — itself a class with potential ocular protective effects.
Limitations
  • Inadequate capture of NAION from ICD-10 diagnosis codes alone may introduce outcome misclassification.
  • Intention-to-treat analysis based on initial prescription dilutes true on-treatment effects through medication discontinuation and switching.
  • Residual confounding remains plausible despite overlap weighting — particularly for unmeasured factors such as disease duration, adherence, and inflammatory biomarkers.
  • Substantial baseline imbalances across cohorts (age, sex, BMI, comorbidity burden) reflect distinct prescribing patterns; weighting balances measured but not unmeasured covariates.
  • Retrospective design precludes causal inference, and absolute event rates may be overestimated due to variable follow-up duration.

Proposed Mechanisms
🔬
Anti-Inflammatory
GLP-1RAs reduce IL-6, IL-1β, and TNF-α — key cytokines implicated in ocular surface inflammation and DED pathogenesis.
💧
Lacrimal Gland
GLP-1 receptors are expressed in lacrimal gland and ocular adnexal tissues. Topical liraglutide improved tear secretion in diabetic murine models; human studies report higher Schirmer values and longer TBUT.
⚖️
Metabolic & Lifestyle
GLP-1RA-associated weight loss and lifestyle modification may reduce DED severity. The stronger signal in non-diabetic patients — where hyperglycemia is absent — supports this as an important driver.
Limitations
  • ICD-10 code-based DED classification may introduce misclassification bias, as dry eye diagnoses are clinically heterogeneous and often assigned by exclusion.
  • Despite propensity matching, BMI and HbA1c remained imbalanced (SMD >0.10) across cohorts, indicating potential residual confounding by metabolic severity.
  • Confounding by indication is plausible — GLP-1RAs, insulin, metformin, and SGLT2i are prescribed to distinct patient profiles with different baseline risk.
  • Unmeasured confounders (disease duration, adherence, inflammatory biomarkers, socioeconomic status, concurrent immunomodulatory therapy) were not captured.
  • No clinical exam metrics were available (TBUT, tear osmolarity, meibography, corneal esthesiometry), limiting clinical granularity.
  • Retrospective design precludes causal inference. An ophthalmology encounter as the index event may introduce immortal time bias.
